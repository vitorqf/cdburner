# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Two people: the developer who built it, and his girlfriend who uses it to play CDs on a radio player.
The girlfriend's context: she's not a technical user. She picks music she loves, presses burn, and holds a physical disc in her hands.
The developer's context: building a tracklist, managing imports, queuing a burn — he's in control mode, but not hurrying.
This is a domestic tool. It lives on a home network and is used in a relaxed, intentional setting.

## Product Purpose

CDburner is a self-hosted web service for building and burning audio CDs from file uploads and YouTube URLs.
It runs on a homelab with a physical CD drive. It does one thing: take music you love and put it on a disc you can hold.
Success looks like: your girlfriend sits down, picks songs, burns a CD, and walks away with something real.

## Brand Personality

Intimate, Considered, Calm.

Warm and personal — this was made for one person, not shipped to thousands.
Every interaction should feel like a small act of care, not a task in a workflow.

## Anti-references

- **Generic music streaming UI** (Spotify, Apple Music clones): green or purple sidebars, streaming-service chrome, grid of album cards that looks like a store. This is a making tool, not a discovery tool.
- **Dev tool / admin panel aesthetic**: monospace fonts, dark utility dashboards, terminal vibes. The girlfriend isn't a developer.
- **Corporate SaaS dashboard**: metric cards, data tables, nav that looks like enterprise software.
- **Skeuomorphic retro**: fake CD player UI, brushed metal, heavy textures trying to mimic physical media. Nostalgia through craft, not costume.

## Design Principles

1. **A gift, not a product.** Every screen should feel like it was made for one specific person. No generic affordances, no platform-think. The interface knows who's using it.

2. **The occasion matters.** Burning a CD is a deliberate act of care. The tool should honor the weight of that — unhurried, no notifications, no dark patterns nudging you anywhere.

3. **Physical media, digital craft.** The warmth of analog music culture should come through in texture, type, and color choices — not as costume (fake CD player UI) but as emotional resonance.

4. **Considered over complete.** Every screen has one clear job. Features earn their place. The tool does less, better.

5. **Calm is a feature.** No urgency, no gamification, no progress-bar anxiety. The interface waits for you, not the other way around.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

No formal WCAG target — this is a personal tool for two known users.
Still: sufficient contrast, comfortable text sizing, no interaction that relies on color alone.
Keyboard navigation is nice-to-have, not required.
